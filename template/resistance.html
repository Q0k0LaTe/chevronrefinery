Resistance is Fertile

Richmond is not just a victim; it is a leader in the Environmental Justice movement.


Asian Pacific Environmental Network (APEN)

APEN began in 1993. They organize Laotian refugee families who live in North Richmond.

These families lived in cheap housing inside the refinery pollution zone. APEN workers knock on doors, speak many languages, and help people speak at government meetings.

APEN has won important changes:

  • Stronger pollution rules
  • Air monitors that show pollution in real time
  • Emergency alerts in many languages

Richmond Progressive Alliance (RPA)

RPA started in 2003. It is a group of local people who want to reduce Chevron's power.

For many years, Chevron gave money to City Council members. The council did whatever Chevron wanted.

RPA ran candidates who refused oil money. They won elections. Now people at City Council meetings asked for stricter rules, not automatic approval for Chevron.

RPA leaders, like Mayor Gayle McLaughlin and Eduardo Martinez, changed many policies:

  • Higher taxes for Chevron
  • Better safety checks
  • Agreements for Chevron to help the community

They showed that people can win against a huge company.

Communities for a Better Environment (CBE)

CBE uses law, organizing, and youth leadership.

Their lawyers have sued Chevron many times. They forced the refinery to install better pollution controls.

CBE also trains young people from Richmond. These students speak at City Council, run air monitors, and protest outside the refinery.

Community air monitors gave real data. Now when Chevron says a fire was "small," residents can check themselves and prove the truth.


Important Moments

2012 Refinery Fire

A big fire sent toxic smoke over Richmond. 15,000 people went to the hospital. This event made many people angry and created strong organizing.

Rule 6-5

After years of pressure, the air district made strong limits on refinery pollution. Chevron fought this for many years but lost.

Elections

RPA candidates won City Council seats. This changed the city's politics.

Community Benefits

Chevron now has to spend money on:

  • clinics
  • job training
  • green projects

These projects help people who live closest to the pollution.

"We Are Not a Sacrifice Zone"

Richmond organizers say this: We are not a place for others to throw away. Our lives are not less important.

They connect environmental health with:

  • housing
  • immigrant rights
  • labor rights
  • democracy

People who live near the pollution are not just victims. They are leaders and experts.

They speak many languages, collect data, go to meetings, and run elections. Richmond shows how a community can fight the long history of redlining and industrial racism.

As one APEN organizer said:

"They put the refinery next to us because they thought we wouldn't fight back. They were wrong."
